Children's Convalescent Trips

Saturday, 08 November 2025

15:33

 

 

The phenomenon of "Kinderverschickungen" (Children's Cures/Trips) after the post-war period, which extended well into the 1970s and early 1980s in West Germany (some sources cite the period from the late 1940s to the 1980s), affected millions of children who were sent to places like the North Sea, the Black Forest, or the Allgäu for a so-called "Erholungskur" (convalescent cure/trip).

 

Dormitory in a children’s cure home

 

 

 

 

Background of the Children's Trips

 

Medical-Social Necessity (Early Years): The trips were initiated in the post-war period to "fatten up" undernourished or sick children, often from low-income families in urban and industrial areas. The cure was intended to strengthen the children through fresh air (e.g., the North Sea climate), healthy nutrition, and a structured daily routine.

 

Economic Miracle Boom: In later years, the cures were often paid for by health insurance funds (Krankenkassen) or social welfare offices and were considered a standard measure for recovery and preventative healthcare, often even when parents themselves had to go to a cure.

 

Tradition: The practice of "Landverschickung" (sending children to the countryside) had precedents, including during the Nazi era (evacuation to the countryside), and the authoritarian camp-style education was conserved in some cure homes until the 1970s.

 

 

Age Group and Circumstances

 

Age: Toddlers up to adolescents were affected, with children of primary school age (from about 4-6 years) frequently being sent for cures in the 1970s and 1980s.

 

Duration: Stays typically lasted four to six weeks.

 

Circumstances: The children were usually transported in groups, often by train, to the recreational areas. The separation from the family was often abrupt and traumatic for the children.

 

 

Accommodation and Daily Life

 

Accommodation: The children lived in special children's cure homes or convalescent homes, operated by various organizations (e.g., welfare associations, church organizations).

 

Daily Routine: The daily life was characterized by a strict, military-like regimen. There was an emphasis on order, discipline, and often a commanding tone.

 

  • Mandatory activities included afternoon naps, daily excursions (often to the sea), and specific behavioral rules.

 

  • Children were often treated not as individuals but as a mass to be managed and had to conform to the routines ("to be brought into line").

 

 

Problems, Abuse, and Mistreatment

 

Despite the original goal of recovery, countless children experienced the cure as a trauma due to systematic violence, humiliation, and mistreatment. The problems often resulted from structural shortcomings, unqualified and underpaid staff, and an authoritarian educational philosophy prevalent at the time.

 

Type of Mistreatment

Examples from Victims' Reports

Forced Feeding

Children were forced to eat all their food—even if they vomited it up. Refusal resulted in punishments, such as sitting in front of the plate for hours.

Physical Violence

Hitting (e.g., with wooden sandals for not sleeping), pulling hair, locking them in dark cellars. Fatalities attributed to violence or neglect have also been documented.

Psychological Violence

Humiliations (e.g., standing naked, spanking the bare bottom while brushing teeth), yelling, isolation, being addressed by a number instead of a name, insults.

Neglect

Lack of hygiene (e.g., no changing of underwear or bedding), no comfort for homesickness, isolation when sick.

Control and Punishment

Vetting of letters to parents (homesickness was not allowed to be mentioned), prohibition of sweets, punishments such as standing in the corner for hours or exclusion from games/excursions.

Sexual Abuse

Sexual assaults and abuse by staff were also documented in victims' reports.

 

The suffering experienced has led to lifelong psychological trauma such as anxiety disorders, eating disorders, and loss of trust in many "Verschickungskinder".

Today, there are initiatives for the clarification and reappraisal of these events.
